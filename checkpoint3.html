1. App Motor Control & Bluetooth Challenges

For Checkpoint 3, the team‚Äôs primary focus was getting reliable motor control from the mobile app through Bluetooth. Initially, all Bluetooth work was done in C, and the team successfully connected to the Raspberry Pi. However, none of the motor control commands could be transmitted reliably from the phone to the Pi within the C-only solution.

After running into repeated issues, the team asked Dr. Herring on November 4, 2025, whether Python could be used for the Bluetooth connection while keeping the rest of the project in C. The initial email response allowed the use of Python on the Raspberry Pi for Bluetooth, with the constraint that the phone and RP4 must be able to reconnect after forgetting the device. A later follow-up email on November 6, 2025, clarified that if Bluetooth remained in Python and the rest of the embedded code stayed in C, the project would not receive full credit; the professor emphasized that other groups had achieved a C-based setup.

This put the team at a disadvantage because those groups used Android/Windows, while this team used an iPhone/Mac stack. By talking with another iOS/Mac group (Gaelan & Blake), they confirmed that the C-based approach was also failing there. The key technical difference turned out to be the underlying Bluetooth profiles: other teams relied on RFCOMM/Serial Port Profile, which is not supported for third-party iOS apps.

RFCOMM vs BLE GATT

Through extensive AI-assisted research and experimentation, the team verified that RFCOMM is simple to implement but incompatible with standard iOS apps, while BLE GATT is more complex but fully supported and is the industry standard for BLE. In the CP3 report:

  • Figure 1 highlights the conceptual differences between BLE GATT and RFCOMM.
  • Figure 2 compares how an Android phone can easily use RFCOMM, while iOS requires GATT for third-party apps.
  • Figure 3 contrasts the code complexity: a short RFCOMM socket sequence versus a multi-step BLE GATT flow involving services, characteristics, BlueZ daemons, and D-Bus messaging.

Despite these constraints, the team persisted‚Äîiterating with multiple AIs, testing alternative code paths, and documenting the incompatibilities. This checkpoint represents the turning point where the team began narrowing the solution toward a BLE-centric design while still honoring the project‚Äôs C-based requirements as much as possible.
